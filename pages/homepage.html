Reframing Collections: From Geographic Generalizations to Cultural Context

（TBD）Reframing Collections addresses a critical gap in how museums present and contextualize their collections. Currently, institutions like the Detroit Institute of Arts organize diverse cultural artifacts under broad geographic categories such as "Asian Art," which homogenizes distinct cultures, materials, time periods, and artistic traditions. This approach removes objects from their specific cultural contexts and reinforces problematic generalizations. Our project creates a digital tool that enables museums to present their collections through culturally-situated networks that include geographic connections alongside cultural context. Rather than relying solely on broad regional categories like "Asian Art," we connect objects based on materials, themes, techniques, cultural practices, AND meaningful geographic relationships, which should provide visitors with a more nuanced and respectful understanding of artistic traditions that honors both cultural specificity and geographic heritage. This intervention is both educationally significant and serves anti-racist museum practices by moving away from orientalist categorization systems. This project addresses the urgent need for museums to decolonize their curatorial practices while providing practical implementation guidance for institutional change.

Deliverables

(TBD)We are creating an interactive web-based platform and mobile app that will feature a profile mode, showcasing detailed 2D/3D models of objects with interactive hotspots that provide contextual information beyond physical display limitations. Another dimension is the network map mode, which can be a dynamic visualization showing the connections between objects based on cultural contexts, materials, techniques, themes, and geography. And finally, we have a before-and-after demonstration with clear examples showing the traditional geographic categorization versus the culturally situated organization.

Our Partners

(TBD)Our main institutional partner is the Detroit Institute of Arts(DIA), and this will provide us with the initial data collection from the Asian artworks, and also to get their iterative institutional feedback for improvement. Other potential partners would be additional museums, digital libraries, or archives with contemporary collections for our network expansion. We would also look into cultural organizations and community groups for some authentic contextualization. Also, academic institutions for research validation and student engagement. These partnerships would help us gain access to improved contentualization tools and leadership in museum decolonization efforts.
